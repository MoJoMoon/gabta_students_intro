Most AI tools you'll use day-to-day are Generative AI - they help you create text, solve problems, and generate content. Embodied and Agentic AI are emerging but not yet common on most job sites.

üîí On-Site Data Privacy

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule for Trade Workers

Never put student names, personal information, or sensitive project details into AI tools. When in doubt, use placeholders and general descriptions.

üõ°Ô∏è 3-Step Privacy Check

  1. Strip names/identifiers: Remove any personal info that could identify students or private clients
  2. Use placeholders: "Student A," "Project Site," "Client Property" instead of real names
  3. Save locally first: Review AI output before sharing to catch any unexpected personal details

üìã What's Protected (FERPA)

  • Student names and addresses
  • Grades and test scores
  • Attendance records
  • Disciplinary information
  • Photos with identifiable students
  • Family/financial information

ü§ñ Embodied AI: Physical Work & Robots

üéØ AI That Moves and Works in the Physical World

Embodied AI combines artificial intelligence with physical bodies - robots that can sense, move, and perform tasks in real environments like construction sites.

ü¶æ Humanoid Robots

Built in human form for human tasks

  • Can use standard tools and equipment
  • Navigate spaces designed for humans
  • Perform repetitive or dangerous tasks
  • Examples: Atlas (Boston Dynamics), Tesla Bot
Construction Use: Moving materials, basic assembly, working in hazardous environments

üêï Quadruped Robots

Four-legged bots for patrol and search/rescue

  • Stable movement over rough terrain
  • Carry sensors and cameras
  • Autonomous site monitoring
  • Examples: Spot (Boston Dynamics), ANYmal
Construction Use: Site security, progress monitoring, hazard detection

üöÅ Drones

Aerial mobility for inspections, mapping, delivery

  • Access hard-to-reach areas safely
  • Real-time site monitoring
  • Precision measurements and mapping
  • Material delivery to specific locations
Construction Use: Roof inspections, surveying, progress documentation

ü§ñ Collaborative Robots (Cobots)

Stationary or semi-mobile arms that work with humans

  • Designed to work safely alongside people
  • Assist with lifting and precision tasks
  • Can be taught new tasks easily
  • Examples: Universal Robots, ABB YuMi
Construction Use: Heavy lifting, precise placement, repetitive assembly

üöÄ Future of Robots in Construction

  • Near Term (1-3 years): More drones, basic cobots for lifting
  • Medium Term (3-7 years): Humanoids for simple assembly tasks
  • Long Term (7+ years): Fully autonomous construction crews
  • Always: Humans still needed for complex problem-solving and creativity

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety & Privacy Considerations

  • Worker Safety: Proper training on human-robot interaction
  • Data Privacy: Robots with cameras may record workers
  • Job Security: Understanding which tasks robots will/won't replace
  • Maintenance: New skills needed to service robotic equipment

üéØ Bottom Line for Trade Workers

Robots are becoming co-workers, not replacements. They'll handle dangerous, repetitive, or physically demanding tasks while humans focus on skilled work, problem-solving, and creativity. Start learning about them now to stay ahead!

üí¨ Your First AI Prompt

üéØ Time to Try AI Yourself!

Let's practice writing prompts for real trade situations. Start with something simple and practical for your work.

üìù Good Prompt Structure

  • Be specific: "Calculate materials for 32√ó24 wall" not "help with math"
  • Give context: "I'm a construction student" helps AI tailor responses
  • Ask for format: "Show step-by-step" or "give me a checklist"
  • Include constraints: "Using standard 2√ó4 lumber" adds important details

üöÄ Starter Prompt Ideas

  • Math Help: "Convert 7‚Öú inches to decimal and explain the steps"
  • Safety Info: "Create a 5-point ladder safety checklist"

  • Materials: "What's the difference between 16d and 8d nails?"

‚úçÔ∏è Write Your First Prompt

üîÑ Common AI Tools to Try
